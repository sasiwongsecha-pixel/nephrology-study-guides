Hyponatremia
SIADH — Bartter-Schwartz Criteria

All 5 must be present · Source: Brenner Ch. 14

  • Hypoosmolality: Plasma Osm < 275 mOsm/kg
  • Inappropriately concentrated urine: Uosm > 100 mOsm/kg (not maximally dilute)
  • Clinical euvolemia: No edema, no orthostatic hypotension
  • Elevated urine Na⁺: UNa > 30 mmol/L (on normal intake)
  • Exclusion: No adrenal, thyroid, cardiac, hepatic, or renal disease; no diuretics

🔍 Clue: Hypouricemia (<4 mg/dL) — increased urate clearance is a frequent diagnostic tip

Hyponatremia — Causes by Volume

Hypovolemic (UNa <30 → extrarenal; UNa >30 → renal)

  • Vomiting/diarrhea (UNa <30), diuretics, Addison's (UNa >30)

Euvolemic (Uosm >100 → impaired dilution)

  • SIADH (most common), hypothyroidism, cortisol deficiency, primary polydipsia (Uosm <100)

Hypervolemic (UNa <30 → Na retention)

  • Heart failure, cirrhosis (splanchnic vasodilation → neurohormonal activation), nephrotic syndrome
Treatment — Correction Limits & ODS Risk

Source: Brenner Ch. 14 · Brain adapts by losing organic osmolytes — rapid correction risks ODS/CPM

  • Standard: Max 10–12 mmol/L in 24h; 18 mmol/L in 48h
  • High ODS risk: Max 8 mmol/L in 24h

⚠ High ODS Risk Factors:

  • Baseline Na⁺ ≤ 105 mmol/L
  • Hypokalemia (concurrent)
  • Alcoholism / malnutrition
  • Advanced liver disease

✅ Overcorrection rescue:

  • 5% Dextrose water + DDAVP to re-lower serum Na⁺ to safe level

⚡ Acute hyponatremia (<48h): ODS not reported — rapid correction safer, symptomatic Tx with 3% NaCl 1–2 mL/kg bolus

🔬 Diagnostic workup (both)
  • Serum: Na, K, Cl, HCO₃, BUN, Cr, glucose, Ca, uric acid
  • Plasma + urine osmolality (paired)
  • Spot urine Na, K, Cr, uric acid
  • TSH, AM cortisol (rule out hypothyroid / adrenal insufficiency before SIADH)
  • Daily weights + strict I/O
  • Orthostatic vital signs; consider CVP / IVC ultrasound
  • BNP/ANP if available — useful if elevated
  • Imaging: CNS source (CT/MRI) + chest CT (SCLC) as indicated
💊 SIADH treatment
  • Fluid restriction 800–1000 mL/day (cornerstone)
  • Treat underlying cause (drug, malignancy, infection)
  • Salt tablets + loop diuretic if mild/chronic
  • Urea 15–30 g/day if free-water restriction not tolerated
  • Tolvaptan 15 mg/day (V2R antagonist) for resistant SIADH; monitor liver enzymes
  • 3% NaCl 100 mL bolus × 1–3 only for severe symptoms (seizure, coma) — target ↑Na 4–6 mEq/L in 6h
  • If overshoot >10 mEq/24h: D5W + DDAVP 2–4 mcg IV to re-lower Na
💊 CSW treatment
  • Volume + salt replacement — NEVER fluid restrict
  • Isotonic saline 1.5–3 mL/kg/h initially, titrate to euvolemia
  • Oral salt tablets 6–12 g/day
  • Fludrocortisone 0.1–0.4 mg/day PO — best-supported adjunct, particularly post-SAH
  • 3% NaCl 100 mL bolus only for severe symptoms — target same ↑Na 4–6 mEq/L in 6h
  • Maintain CVP >5 cmH₂O, MAP >70 mmHg (cerebral perfusion in SAH)
  • Re-evaluate every 4–6h — once Na/volume corrected, taper fludrocortisone over 2–4 weeks
⚠ Common pitfalls:
  • Diagnosing SIADH without checking volume status → missing CSW → fatal cerebral ischemia from fluid restriction.
  • Aggressive 3% NaCl when chronic ↓Na (>48 h) → osmotic demyelination (ODS / CPM). Cap correction at 8–10 mEq/L per 24 h.
  • Forgetting to rule out hypothyroidism + adrenal insufficiency before labeling SIADH.
  • Giving isotonic saline to a true SIADH with very high Uosm — the kidney retains free water and excretes Na, paradoxically dropping serum Na further.
Hypernatremia
Causes of Hypernatremia

Inadequate water intake

  • Altered thirst/consciousness, no access to water (elderly, ICU)

Excessive water loss

  • Renal: Diabetes Insipidus (central or nephrogenic), osmotic diuresis (hyperglycemia, mannitol)
  • Extrarenal: Diarrhea, sweating, burns (hypotonic loss)

Na⁺ gain

  • Hypertonic saline, NaHCO₃, mineralocorticoid excess
Diabetes Insipidus — Central vs Nephrogenic

Both → Uosm <200 mOsm/kg (very dilute urine)

Feature Central DI Nephrogenic DI
Defect ADH deficiency ADH resistance
Causes Pituitary surgery, head trauma, craniopharyngioma Lithium, hypercalcemia, hypokalemia, V2R/AQP2 mutations
DDAVP response Uosm ↑↑ (>750) Minimal / absent
Treatment Desmopressin (DDAVP) Low-Na diet + thiazide + indomethacin; remove cause

Free water deficit = TBW × (Serum Na / 140 − 1) where TBW = 0.6 × wt (♂) or 0.5 × wt (♀). Correct ≤0.5 mEq/L/h, ≤8–10 mEq/L/24h.

Approach to Hyponatremia

3-step systematic algorithm · Source: Brenner Ch. 14

1 Measure Plasma Osmolality — Is the hyponatremia real?
Normal Posm (275–295)

Pseudohyponatremia — Hyperlipidemia or hyperproteinemia displacing water fraction. Lab artifact (flame photometry). No treatment needed.

High Posm (>295)

Translocational hyponatremia — Effective osmole (glucose, mannitol) draws water out of cells. Correct by 1.6 mEq/Na per 100 mg/dL glucose above 100.

Low Posm (<275)

TRUE hyponatremia — Proceed to Step 2 ↓

2 Assess ECF Volume Status — What is the sodium balance?
Hypovolemic

↓ Total body Na > ↓ TBW. Signs: orthostatic hypotension, tachycardia, dry mucosa. → Step 3 to find renal vs extrarenal losses

Euvolemic

TBW slightly ↑, Na normal. No edema, no dehydration. Most likely SIADH, hypothyroidism, cortisol deficiency, primary polydipsia.

Hypervolemic

↑ Total body Na AND TBW (TBW > Na). Edema present. Heart failure, cirrhosis, nephrotic syndrome.

3 Urine Osmolality + Urine Sodium — Why can't they dilute?
Uosm < 100 mOsm/kg

Maximally dilute — diluting ability intact. Primary polydipsia (beer potomania, tea-and-toast diet). No ADH effect.

Uosm > 100 + Hypovolemic

UNa <30: Extrarenal losses (vomiting, diarrhea, 3rd spacing). UNa >30: Renal losses (diuretics, Addison's, cerebral salt-wasting).

Uosm > 100 + Euvolemic

UNa >30: SIADH (most common). Rule out hypothyroid/adrenal. Consider SIADH subtype (A–D).

Uosm > 100 + Hypervolemic

UNa <10: HF or cirrhosis (avid Na retention). UNa >20: CKD/renal impairment.

Approach to Hypernatremia
1 Measure Urine Osmolality — What is the concentrating ability?
Uosm > 700 mOsm/kg

Kidney concentrating normally — Loss is extrarenal (sweating, GI) or inadequate water intake. Urine Na helps distinguish Na overload.

Uosm 200–700 mOsm/kg

Partial concentrating defect — Partial DI, osmotic diuresis (glucosuria, post-obstruction). Mixed picture.

Uosm < 200 mOsm/kg

Severe concentrating defect — Diabetes Insipidus (central or nephrogenic). Proceed to DDAVP test.

2 DDAVP Test — Differentiate Central vs Nephrogenic DI
Uosm ↑ >750 after DDAVP

Central DI — ADH deficient. Treat with intranasal / oral desmopressin. Check MRI pituitary.

Uosm minimal change (<50% ↑)

Nephrogenic DI — V2R or AQP2 defect. Remove lithium/hypercalcemia. Thiazide + indomethacin + low-Na diet.

3 Correct Free Water Deficit Safely

Free Water Deficit = TBW × (Serum Na / 140 − 1)

TBW = 0.6 × wt kg (♂) or 0.5 × wt kg (♀) or 0.45 (elderly ♀)

Rate: ≤ 0.5 mEq/L/h and ≤ 8–10 mEq/L in 24h. Use D5W, ½ NS, or oral water.

⚠ Cerebral edema risk if corrected too fast in chronic hypernatremia (>48h).

    Study Guide

    Concise, high-yield summary of CKD and AKI management from Brenner & Rector's 12th Edition.

    KDIGO CGA Classification (2012, reaffirmed 2024)

    CKD = structural or functional kidney abnormality persisting ≥3 months. Classified by Cause, GFR category, and Albuminuria category.

    GFR Categories (G1–G5):

    G1 ≥90 · Normal/highG2 60–89 · Mild ↓G3a 45–59 · Mild–mod ↓G3b 30–44 · Mod–severe ↓G4 15–29 · Severe ↓G5 <15 · Kidney failure

    Albuminuria Categories (A1–A3, UACR mg/g):

    A1 <30 · Normal/mildA2 30–300 · ModerateA3 >300 · Severe
    Key thresholds:
    • Global CKD prevalence: 13.4% of adults
    • CKD-MBD monitoring begins at Stage G3
    • Nephrology referral indicated when UACR >300 mg/g or Stage G4
    • RRT planning should begin at Stage G4; initiate KRT when eGFR <10 or uremic symptoms appear

    Pathophysiology of CKD Progression

    Regardless of primary cause, CKD progresses via a vicious cycle of nephron loss:

    • Hemodynamic: Glomerular hypertension and hyperfiltration (remaining nephrons)
    • Hormonal: Angiotensin II and Aldosterone activation → fibrosis
    • Inflammatory: Proteinuria-driven tubulointerstitial fibrosis (TIF) and FSGS

    The Four Pillars of Kidney Protection

    1

    RAAS Inhibitors (ACEi/ARB)

    Indication: UACR ≥30 mg/g
    Mechanism: ↓ efferent tone → ↓ intraglomerular pressure
    Monitor: SCr + K⁺ at 1–2 weeks; accept ≤30% SCr rise
    Avoid: Dual ACEi+ARB (ONTARGET)
    Trials: RENAAL, IDNT, ROAD

    2

    SGLT2 Inhibitors

    T2DM: eGFR ≥20 (any UACR)
    Non-DM: eGFR 20–45 OR eGFR >45 + UACR ≥200
    Expect: Acute eGFR dip 3–5 mL/min (hemodynamic, reversible)
    Benefit: ↓ hyperkalemia with RAASi
    Trials: CREDENCE, DAPA-CKD, EMPA-KIDNEY

    3

    Nonsteroidal MRA (Finerenone)

    Criteria: T2DM + eGFR ≥25 + normal K⁺ + residual albuminuria on RAASi
    vs Spironolactone: Less hyperkalemia (ARTS), no gynecomastia (minimal androgen affinity)
    Trials: FIDELIO-DKD, FIGARO-DKD, ARTS

    4

    GLP-1 RA (Semaglutide)

    Criteria: T2DM + CKD + weight management or high CV risk
    Key result: FLOW trial: 24% ↓ kidney composite; stopped early for efficacy
    Also: Tirzepatide (GIP/GLP-1 co-agonist) shows superior weight loss
    Trials: FLOW

    Proteinuria as a Treatment Target (ROAD Study):

    Titrating ACEi to the maximum antiproteinuric dose (not just BP target) halved ESKF risk vs conventional dosing. Target: UACR <30 mg/g. A 3-month change in proteinuria predicts subsequent GFR decline rate.

    Lifestyle & Dietary Interventions

    • Smoking cessation: Doubles risk of death from ESKF — most avoidable cause of CV mortality. All patients should be counselled.
    • Weight: Target BMI <25 kg/m²; even 10 lb (4.5 kg) loss lowers BP. GLP-1 RA and bariatric surgery for those failing lifestyle alone. Look AHEAD trial: intensive lifestyle intervention → 31% ↓ in high-risk CKD.
    • Sodium: <5 g salt/day (<2.3 g sodium). 80% of dietary sodium comes from food processing — target processed foods, not just table salt. High sodium negates the antiproteinuric effect of ACEi.
    • Protein: Avoid >1.3 g/kg/day; target 0.8 g/kg/day in stages G4–G5 to reduce excretory burden.
    • Exercise: Moderate intensity 30–60 min, 4–7 days/week significantly slows eGFR decline.
    • Metabolic acidosis (diet-first): A diet rich in fruits and vegetables reduces dietary acid load — first-line before pharmacological bicarbonate.

    Complication Management

    Anemia of CKD
    • Diagnosis: Hb <13 g/dL (men), <12 g/dL (women)
    • Cause: ↓ renal EPO production
    • Treat iron first (TSAT <20%, ferritin <100)
    • ESA target Hb: 10–11.5 g/dL
    • Do NOT exceed 13 g/dL (↑ stroke + mortality)
    Metabolic Acidosis
    • Occurs when GFR falls below 40–50 mL/min
    • Drives protein catabolism + bone loss
    • First-line: fruit/vegetable-rich diet
    • Supplement NaHCO₃ when HCO₃⁻ <18 mEq/L
    CKD-MBD
    • First sign: ↑ FGF-23 (earliest biomarker, before PTH)
    • Monitor Ca, Phos, PTH from Stage G3
    • Treatment: dietary phosphate restriction + binders
    • Vitamin D deficiency worsens secondary HPT
    Hyperkalemia
    • Major RAASi and MRA side effect
    • ZS-9 (Patiromer alternative): exchanges K⁺ for Na⁺/H⁺ → edema risk
    • Patiromer: exchanges K⁺ for Ca²⁺ in colon
    • Novel binders allow continuation of RAASi therapy
    Cardiovascular Risk
    • CKD = cardiovascular risk equivalent
    • Statin ≥50 yrs: ALL patients
    • Statin <50 yrs: T2DM, prior stroke, or 10-yr CVD risk >10%
    • BP target (KDIGO 2024): ≤120 mmHg systolic
    Vaccination & Referral
    • Hep B vaccine: do in Stage G3–G4 (seroconversion falls later)
    • Late referral = <3 months before dialysis starts
    • Avoid NSAIDs (AKI risk)
    • Hold RAASi during sick days (vomiting/diarrhea)

    Acute Kidney Injury — Staging & Workup

    KDIGO AKI Staging:

    Stage 1: SCr ×1.5–1.9 OR rise ≥0.3 mg/dL/48h Stage 2: SCr ×2.0–2.9 Stage 3: SCr ×3.0 OR ≥4.0 mg/dL OR KRT initiated
    Prerenal AKI
    • FENa <1%
    • Bland urine sediment
    • High urine SG (>1.020)
    • Urine Na <20 mEq/L
    Intrinsic AKI (ATN)
    • FENa >2%
    • Isosthenuria (SG ~1.010)
    • Muddy-brown granular casts
    • Renal tubular epithelial cells

    $$FE_{Na} = \frac{U_{Na} \times P_{Cr}}{P_{Na} \times U_{Cr}} \times 100\%$$

    Management: Restore perfusion (balanced crystalloids; ROSE phases); avoid fluid overload; avoid nephrotoxins (NSAIDs, aminoglycosides); adjust drug dosing; KRT for urgent indications (AEIOU: Acidosis, Electrolytes, Intoxication, Overload, Uremia). CRRT/IHD/PIRRT preferred; effluent dose 20–25 mL/kg/h; Regional citrate anticoagulation (RCA) first-line.
