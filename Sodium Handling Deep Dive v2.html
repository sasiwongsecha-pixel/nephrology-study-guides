Sodium Handling · Reading Companion

The kidney filters 1.5 kg of salt a day.
It excretes about a teaspoon.

At a GFR of 180 L/day and serum Na⁺ ≈ 140 mmol/L, the glomeruli pour ≈ 25,000 mmol of Na⁺ into the nephron daily — enough to fill ten extracellular-fluid volumes. 99.6% of that filtered load must be clawed back. This is the segment-by-segment story of how it happens, what regulates each step, and what breaks when a single transporter goes wrong.

Two phases, two driving forces

The PT performs near-isosmotic reabsorption of two-thirds to three-quarters of the glomerular ultrafiltrate. In the early S1 segment, Na⁺ entry is coupled to glucose, amino acids, phosphate, and a robust NHE3-driven HCO₃⁻ reabsorption. The electrogenic removal of Na⁺ with these organic solutes generates a lumen-negative PD, which drives paracellular Cl⁻ uptake.

By the mid-to-late PT, luminal HCO₃⁻ and glucose are depleted; relative luminal NaCl concentration rises above peritubular plasma. The PD reverses to lumen-positive, driven by passive Cl⁻ diffusion through claudin-10a / -17. About 40% of late-PT NaCl reabsorption is paracellular.

Transcellular: the NHE3 + anion-exchange machine

Apical Cl⁻ entry is electroneutral, coupled to NHE3 via parallel Cl⁻ / anion exchangers (SLC26A6 with formate, oxalate, or HCO₃⁻). NHE3 knockout mice show a 62% drop in fluid absorption and 54% drop in baseline Cl⁻ absorption — but global salt-wasting is mostly intestinal, not renal.

Glomerulotubular balance: when GFR rises, PT reabsorption rises in lockstep, so fractional reabsorption stays constant. Du et al. showed this is partly mechanical — flow bends microvilli, which activates NHE3 and the H⁺-ATPase via the actin cytoskeleton.

Why "leaky" matters

Freeze-fracture EM shows the PT tight junction has one junctional strand; the DCT has many anastomosing strands. Claudin-2 mediates paracellular Na⁺ + water flux; claudin-10a is the anion-selective pathway. Loss of claudin-2 lowers PT Na⁺ / water absorption and worsens renal ischemia (the loop has to work harder, lowering medullary pO₂).

Regulation in one paragraph

AngII (low concentrations) and α₁-adrenergic activity stimulate NHE3 trafficking via the NHERF-1 scaffold; β-adrenergic activity also stimulates NHE3 (paradoxically, bypassing cAMP's normally inhibitory effect). Dopamine and high-dose AngII inhibit NHE3. PTH, secretin, and glucagon (via cAMP) inhibit. ANP causes natriuresis partly here. Acidosis upregulates NHE3 protein; alkalosis downregulates it.

Passive water exit, period.

The thin descending limb (tDLH) is the kidney's water valve. Function is entirely passive: hypotonic luminal fluid encounters a hypertonic medullary interstitium and water leaves through AQP1 until osmotic equilibrium is reached.

Tubular Na⁺ concentration rises ~4-fold by the bend of the loop in long-looped nephrons — not because Na⁺ was added, but because water was removed. The distal 20% of the descending limb has low water permeability and slightly higher NaCl permeability, setting the stage for the ascending limb's passive equilibration.

Why it matters: No active transport, no ATP cost, and no transporter to mutate. But losing AQP1 collapses the countercurrent system. Aquaporin-1 deficient humans cannot concentrate urine above ~450 mOsm/kg.

The passive equilibration trick

By the bend of the loop, luminal fluid has a high NaCl concentration (thanks to upstream water removal). The thin ascending limb (tALH) capitalises on that chemical gradient: Na⁺ exits paracellularly, Cl⁻ exits transcellularly via CLC-K1 (called CLC-Ka in humans).

Vasopressin activates Cl⁻ transport here via V2 receptors and cAMP, and water deprivation triples-to-quadruples CLC-K1 mRNA. Basolateral Ca²⁺ — through the calcium-sensing receptor — inhibits Cl⁻ and Na⁺ transport, contributing to the polyuria of hypercalcemia.

Why this matters: the tALH is the workhorse of the passive equilibration model of countercurrent multiplication. Without it, the kidney can't generate a corticomedullary osmotic gradient → vasopressin-resistant nephrogenic diabetes insipidus in CLC-K1 KO mice.

The K⁺-recycling trick

Apical entry is via NKCC2 with stoichiometry 1 Na⁺ : 1 K⁺ : 2 Cl⁻. But luminal K⁺ is only ~4 mmol/L — far too low to sustain that cotransport rate alone. The fix: ROMK recycles K⁺ back into the lumen. This recycling generates a lumen-positive PD (~+8 mV) that drags paracellular Na⁺, Ca²⁺, and Mg²⁺ through claudin-16 / -19 pores.

Vasopressin doubles NaCl reabsorption here by switching NKCC2 from K⁺-independent to K⁺-dependent mode — without increasing oxygen consumption, because the extra Na⁺ comes through the paracellular path.

Three NKCC2 isoforms, three Cl⁻ affinities

  • NKCC2-F (medulla, inner stripe) — Km for Cl⁻ ≈ 113 mmol/L · low-affinity, high-capacity
  • NKCC2-A (medulla, outer stripe) — Km ≈ 44.7 mmol/L · intermediate
  • NKCC2-B (cortical TAL, macula densa) — Km ≈ 8.9 mmol/L · high-affinity, low-capacity — senses low-flow / low-Cl⁻ states to release renin

ROMK is two channels

Patch-clamp on TAL apical membranes finds a 30-pS and a 70-pS K⁺ channel — but both disappear in ROMK knockouts. ROMK is the pore subunit of both. The 70-pS channel (which carries ~80% of apical K⁺ conductance) is ROMK assembled with the NHERF scaffold and CFTR — giving it ATP- and glibenclamide-sensitivity.

Paracellular Na⁺, Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺

Roughly 50% of TAL Na⁺ transport is paracellular. The claudin family gates which ion: claudin-10b for Na⁺; claudin-16 + claudin-19 for Ca²⁺ / Mg²⁺. Loss of claudin-16 or -19 → familial hypomagnesemia with hypercalciuria and nephrocalcinosis (FHHNC). Loss of claudin-10 → salt-wasting with hypokalemic alkalosis (the "HELIX-like" Bartter mimic).

The Tamm-Horsfall surprise: uromodulin — the most abundant protein in normal urine — positively modulates NKCC2 and ROMK. UMOD mutations cause ADTKD-UMOD: hyperuricemia, gout, and slow CKD. The TAL's signature protein is also its volume sensor.

NCC: electroneutral, phosphorylation-controlled

Apical entry is via the thiazide-sensitive NCC. NCC is regulated almost entirely by phosphorylation, not by trafficking surges. The phosphorylating kinases are SPAK and OSR1; SPAK / OSR1 are themselves phosphorylated and activated by WNK4 (and by KS-WNK1 assembled into "WNK bodies" during K⁺ depletion).

The chloride-binding paradigm — how DCT senses K⁺

WNK4 binds intracellular Cl⁻ at its catalytic site. High [Cl⁻]i inhibits WNK4; low [Cl⁻]i activates it. The basolateral KIR4.1 / KIR5.1 channel translates plasma [K⁺] into a membrane potential, and that potential controls Cl⁻ exit:

  • Low plasma [K⁺] → KIR4.1/5.1 hyperpolarises the basolateral membrane → Cl⁻ exits via CLC-Kb → [Cl⁻]i falls → WNK4 active → SPAK phosphorylates NCC → ↑ NaCl reabsorption, less Na⁺ to ENaC → K⁺ retained
  • High plasma [K⁺] → membrane depolarised → Cl⁻ accumulates → WNK4 off → NCC dephosphorylated → ↑ Na⁺ to ENaC → K⁺ secreted
The aldosterone paradox solved. Aldosterone is high in both volume depletion (need Na⁺ retained, want K⁺ retained) and hyperkalemia (need K⁺ excreted). The DCT decides which: low K⁺ keeps NCC on and Na⁺ off ENaC; high K⁺ shunts Na⁺ to ENaC for K⁺ wasting.

The FHHt / Gitelman mirror

Gain-of-function in WNKs (or in CUL3 / KLHL3 mutations that fail to degrade them) = FHHt / Gordon syndrome / pseudohypoaldosteronism type II: hypertension, hyperkalemia, hyperchloremic metabolic acidosis, hypercalciuria, suppressed renin and aldosterone. Treats beautifully with thiazides. Loss-of-function in NCC = Gitelman syndrome — the exact mirror image: low BP, hypokalemic alkalosis, hypomagnesemia, hypocalciuria.

And remember: the FHHt mutations that hit CUL3 or KLHL3 are more common than the WNK1/WNK4 mutations themselves — and they cause disease by blocking degradation of WNK4.

ENaC — three subunits, two diseases, one gradient

ENaC is a heterotrimer of α-, β-, and γ-subunits, each with intracellular N- and C-terminal tails and a large glycosylated extracellular loop. Apical Na⁺ entry through ENaC without Cl⁻ creates a lumen-negative PD that drives three downstream processes simultaneously:

  • K⁺ secretion via apical ROMK (steady) and BK (flow-dependent) channels in principal cells
  • Paracellular Cl⁻ reabsorption through claudin-4 / -8 pores
  • Electrogenic H⁺ secretion via type-A intercalated cell H⁺-ATPase

Two mirror diseases

Liddle syndrome (GOF in any of α/β/γ-ENaC, autosomal dominant): mutations disrupt the PPxY motif on the C-terminus, blocking NEDD4-2–mediated ubiquitination → ENaC stays on the membrane longer → HTN, hypokalemia, metabolic alkalosis, suppressed aldosterone. Treat with amiloride or triamterene — not spironolactone (the receptor isn't the problem).

Pseudohypoaldosteronism type I (LOF, recessive): neonatal severe salt-wasting, hypotension, hyperkalemia, acidosis despite massively elevated aldosterone. The receptor is fine; the channel is broken.

The intercalated-cell sidebar

Type-B intercalated cells reabsorb Cl⁻ transcellularly via pendrin (SLC26A4) apical Cl⁻/HCO₃⁻ exchange, paired with apical Na⁺ entry via NDCBE (SLC4A8). The net effect is electroneutral NaCl reabsorption that doesn't drive K⁺ secretion — a way to retain Na⁺ in HCO₃⁻-loaded states without dumping K⁺.

Pendrin KO mice are protected from mineralocorticoid hypertension. Pendrin and ENaC are co-activated by AngII; this is part of why ACE-inhibition lowers BP even when aldo is high.

Aldosterone → SGK1 → NEDD4-2

Aldosterone → mineralocorticoid receptor → induces SGK1 → SGK1 phosphorylates NEDD4-2 → NEDD4-2 can no longer ubiquitinate ENaC → channel accumulates on the apical membrane. 11β-HSD2 converts cortisol to cortisone locally so cortisol cannot activate MR. Loss of 11β-HSD2 = apparent mineralocorticoid excess; ingesting glycyrrhetinic acid in liquorice phenocopies it.

The EABV concept unifies HF, cirrhosis, and nephrotic syndrome under one framework: all are states where the kidney senses a volume deficit it cannot correct because the primary defect is upstream (pump failure, vasodilation, or oncotic pressure loss).

↓ HCO₃⁻ reabsorption
Labs: Non-anion-gap metabolic acidosis, urine pH appropriately acidic when severe, plasma HCO₃⁻ 12–18 mEq/L, hypokalemia, possible Fanconi (glycosuria, aminoaciduria, phosphaturia, uricosuria).

Dent disease
CLCN5 (Dent-1) · OCRL1 (Dent-2)PCT
Endosomal Cl⁻/H⁺ exchange defect
Labs: Low-molecular-weight proteinuria, hypercalciuria, nephrocalcinosis, nephrolithiasis, eventual CKD. X-linked recessive.
Dent-2 has Lowe-syndrome overlap features (mild cognitive, cataracts).
Bartter syndrome (I–V)SLC12A1 · KCNJ1 · CLCNKB · BSND · CASRTAL
"Furosemide-mimetic" salt-wasting
Labs: Hypokalemic metabolic alkalosis, hypochloremia, hypercalciuria ± nephrocalcinosis, hyperreninemic hyperaldosteronism, normal-to-low BP. Antenatal types (I, II) have polyhydramnios + prematurity.
Type II (ROMK) shows transient neonatal hyperkalemia before distal compensation kicks in.
FHHNCCLDN16 / CLDN19TAL
Paracellular Mg²⁺/Ca²⁺ pore loss
Labs: Hypomagnesemia + hypercalciuria + nephrocalcinosis, progressive CKD. CLDN19 form has ocular abnormalities (nystagmus, severe myopia).
Mg²⁺ doesn't respond to oral supplementation as easily as in Gitelman — the leak is upstream.
ADTKD-UMODUMODTAL
Misfolded uromodulin → TAL stress
Labs: Hyperuricemia + early-onset gout, bland urinary sediment, slowly progressive CKD, autosomal dominant family history. Modest concentrating defect.
Think: family history of gout + CKD without proteinuria → check UMOD.
Gitelman syndromeSLC12A3 (NCC)DCT
"Chronic-thiazide" phenotype
Labs: Hypokalemic metabolic alkalosis, hypomagnesemia, hypocalciuria, normal-to-low BP, normal-to-low Cl⁻. Adolescent / adult presentation — fatigue, cramps, occasional tetany.
The hypocalciuria is what separates it from Bartter on labs.
FHHt / GordonWNK1 · WNK4 · CUL3 · KLHL3DCT
Pseudo-aldo II — NCC chronically on
Labs: Hypertension + hyperkalemia + hyperchloremic metabolic acidosis, hypercalciuria, suppressed renin and aldosterone. Mirror image of Gitelman.
Treat with low-dose thiazide — the disease and its drug have the same target.
EAST / SeSAMEKCNJ10 (KIR4.1)DCT

Labs: Gitelman-like biochemistry — hypokalemic alkalosis, hypomagnesemia, hypocalciuria — plus Epilepsy, Ataxia, Sensorineural deafness, tubulopathy.

Labs: Early HTN, hypokalemic metabolic alkalosis, suppressed renin AND aldosterone. Autosomal dominant family history of stroke / early HTN.
Use amiloride — not spironolactone. The disease is downstream of MR.

Labs: Neonatal salt-wasting, hypotension, hyperkalemia, metabolic acidosis with markedly elevated aldosterone & renin. AR form is severe, multi-organ; AD form renal-limited and milder.

Cirrhosis / hepatorenal physiology
Splanchnic vasodilation → low EABVwhole nephron
Mechanism: Effective arterial blood volume falls despite total ECF expansion → maximal RAAS + sympathetic + AVP activation → avid Na⁺ retention at every segment.
FeNa < 1%, urinary Na⁺ < 10 mmol/L, ascites with normal-or-low BP — pathognomonic.
Heart failure
↓ Cardiac output → ↓ EABVwhole nephron
Mechanism: Reduced cardiac output → baroreceptor unloading → RAAS + sympathetic + AVP activation → ECF expansion (edema) despite renal Na⁺ avidity.
SGLT2-i + MRA + loop diuretic — three Na⁺ targets stacked.
Nephrotic syndrome
"Overfill" plasmin activates ENaCCNT/CCD
Mechanism: Filtered plasminogen → activated to plasmin in tubular lumen → cleaves γ-ENaC at its inhibitory tract → constitutive ENaC activation → Na⁺ retention even with normal volume sensing.
Amiloride is uniquely effective in nephrotic edema — bypasses the plasmin-activated ENaC.
