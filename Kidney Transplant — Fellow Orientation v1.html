§ 01

Why transplant beats dialysis

The single most important fact for a new fellow: kidney transplantation extends life and improves it compared to staying on dialysis. Everything else in this guide is operational detail on how to deliver that outcome safely.

Core (always visible)
For a comparable ESRD patient, transplantation reduces long-term mortality by ~50-70% vs. remaining on the dialysis waitlist (Wolfe et al, NEJM 1999 — landmark; USRDS reports confirm year-on-year). Quality of life is higher across virtually every domain. In most healthcare systems, transplant becomes cost-saving vs. dialysis by year 2-3.

Why the mortality benefit — the survival-time argument

The landmark Wolfe 1999 analysis compared three matched groups within the USRDS: (1) patients on the deceased-donor waitlist who never received a transplant, (2) those who were transplanted, (3) those who could have been listed but weren't. After accounting for selection, the transplanted group had ~68% lower long-term mortality than the matched waitlist patients. The benefit accrued over years, not months — and the early post-tx period (first ~3-6 months) actually carries higher mortality than dialysis due to surgical and infection risk. The curves cross around month 3-6 post-tx.

Practical message: the patient earns the survival benefit by surviving the early peri-operative period. Picking the right candidate (KDIGO 2020 evaluation) and managing the first 6 months tightly is how that benefit gets realized.

QoL and return-to-function

Transplant recipients report higher SF-36 scores, fewer functional limitations, fewer hospitalizations, and are far more likely to return to work than dialysis-dependent patients. The dietary and fluid liberalization alone is transformative for many. Sexual function, cognitive function, and sleep quality all improve. The flip side: lifelong immunosuppression, surveillance burden, and the psychological weight of graft loss risk.

🇹🇭
Thai context
The Thai Transplantation Society explicitly frames KT as the preferred treatment for ESRD and emphasizes pre-emptive living-donor transplantation as the highest-yield strategy (TTC 2/3). Reason: "the problem of kidney and organ shortage is a major issue in Thailand... although there are many brain-death cases from accidents, the number of organ donors remains low" (Prof. Kriang Tantiwongse, TTC 2). The fellow's job often starts with identifying candidates early enough for a living-donor evaluation.
✓ Check yourself
A 58-year-old on hemodialysis asks "Why bother with transplant — won't the surgery and rejection drugs just trade one set of problems for another?" What's the 30-second answer?
"In the first 3-6 months after transplant your risk of dying is actually slightly higher than if you stayed on dialysis — that's the surgery and the first wave of infections. But after that, your survival curve crosses dialysis and stays better — roughly half the long-term mortality. You'll feel better, eat and drink more freely, and most patients return to work. The trade is real but the math favors transplant unless you're a very high surgical risk or have active cancer/infection."
§ 02

Donor types — who can give a kidney

Two big buckets: deceased donors (DDKT) and living donors (LDKT). Sub-categories within each matter for outcome expectations, surveillance intensity, and (in Thailand) legal eligibility.

Core (always visible)
Living donor (LDKT) > Deceased donor (DDKT) for graft survival and patient survival, at every time point. Living donor kidneys also have shorter cold ischemia, less delayed graft function, and the ability to transplant pre-emptively (before dialysis is needed) — the highest-survival path of all. In Thailand, the deceased-donor pool is small (TTC explicitly cites this as a national bottleneck), so LDKT carries an even larger share of the program than in US/EU.
Deceased donor sub-types — SCD vs ECD vs DCD

SCD — Standard Criteria Donor. <60 years old, no major comorbidities, donation after brain death (DBD). Default expectation; ~75-80% 5-yr graft survival.

ECD — Extended Criteria Donor (Port et al, Transplantation 2002;74:1281). >60 years OR 50-59 years with ≥2 of: hypertension, terminal creatinine >1.5 mg/dL, CVA as cause of death. Higher risk of delayed graft function and shorter graft half-life vs SCD — but still better than waiting on dialysis for many candidates. Often allocated to older recipients (age-matching).

DCD — Donation after Circulatory Death (formerly "non-heart-beating donation"). Donor declared dead by circulatory criteria after planned withdrawal of life-sustaining treatment. Warm ischemia time is the critical variable. Higher DGF rate (~2× DBD) but comparable 1- and 5-year graft survival in experienced centers. Uncommon in Thailand (cultural/regulatory factors).

OPTN replaced the binary SCD/ECD classification with KDPI (Kidney Donor Profile Index) in 2014 — a 0-100% continuous scale combining donor age, history, and function. KDPI 0-20% = highest quality; >85% = highest risk. ECD nomenclature remains in widespread clinical use.

Living donor categories

Genetically related — sibling, parent, child, niece/nephew, cousin. HLA matching is statistically better; emotional motivation is usually clear.

Emotionally related (unrelated) — spouse, partner, close friend. HLA matching is typically poorer but outcomes remain excellent in the modern IS era (the old "HLA-matching matters above all" view has weakened).

Paired kidney exchange (PKE / KPD) — when a willing donor is HLA- or ABO-incompatible with their intended recipient, they "swap" with another such pair so both pairs get compatible transplants. Programs include simple 2-way swaps up to multi-pair chains (sometimes triggered by an altruistic donor).

ABO-incompatible (ABO-i) LDKT — performed with desensitization (PLEX + IVIG ± rituximab) to lower isohaemagglutinin titres. Outcomes approach ABO-compatible LDKT in experienced centers; rare in Thailand outside major centers.

Altruistic / non-directed — anonymous donor with no specific recipient. Common starting point for paired-exchange chains in the US/UK; legally restricted in Thailand (donor-recipient relationship required).

🇹🇭
Thai practice (TTC 2)

Eligible Thai living donors fall into specific legal categories (Medical Council 2010 + Thai Red Cross Organ Donation Centre):

  • Genetic relatives — parents, children, siblings, half-siblings, uncles/aunts, nephews/nieces, cousins (DNA/HLA proof required for extended relatives; TTC 2 p.11 does not explicitly restrict the cousin degree — confirm second-cousin cases with your center's TTC liaison).
  • Legal spouses — marriage certificate + ≥3 years of marriage, waived if biological children together.
  • Foreign nationals — embassy / MFA certification of relationship; DNA/HLA verification at Thai state institution.

Altruistic and unrelated-non-spousal donation is not permitted in routine Thai practice — to prevent commercialization. Paired exchange exists but is uncommon.

✓ Check yourself
Your patient's 64-year-old cousin volunteers as a donor. eGFR 75 mL/min/1.73 m² by CKD-EPI 2009. Per TTC 2, can they donate?
Maybe — borderline. Two issues: (1) Cousin relationship is eligible (extended genetic relative) but requires DNA or HLA proof of relationship. (2) eGFR 75 is in the TTC "70-89" window — requires detailed risk-benefit discussion, consideration of age + comorbidities + willingness to follow long-term monitoring. Many programs proceed to measured GFR (Iohexol or 99mTc-DTPA) to confirm before clearing.
Why does Thailand have proportionally more LDKT than the US?
The Thai deceased-donor pool is small. TTC explicitly cites that "although there are many brain-death cases from accidents, the number of organ donors remains low" — driven by cultural/religious factors, family consent dynamics, and lower hospital-based donor identification rates. The living-donor program (TTC 2) is the operational response.

§ 04

Immunology in 30 seconds

Three sentences to carry into rounds tomorrow. Detail in the HLA and Immunosuppression deep-dive tabs.

Core (always visible) — the 30-second immunology
① Recognition. The donor kidney expresses HLA molecules that look "non-self" to recipient T-cells. T-cells recognize the foreign HLA (directly, on donor APCs) or indirectly (peptides from donor HLA presented by recipient APCs).

② Activation. T-cell activation requires three signals: TCR-HLA engagement (signal 1), co-stimulation via CD28-CD80/86 (signal 2), and cytokines IL-2/IL-15 (signal 3). Every maintenance immunosuppressant blocks one of these signals — that's why "triple therapy" works.

③ Antibody arm. B-cells make donor-specific antibodies (DSA) — a distinct attack mode. T-cell-mediated rejection (TCMR) is more common and responds well to steroids/rATG. Antibody-mediated rejection (ABMR) is harder to treat and drives most late graft loss.
How the 3 signals map to the drugs
  • Signal 1 (TCR-HLA): No direct blocker in routine use. rATG and alemtuzumab deplete T-cells outright, removing the cells that would generate signal 1.
  • Signal 2 (co-stimulation): Blocked by belatacept (CTLA4-Ig fusion → blocks CD28-CD80/86). Without signal 2, signal 1 alone causes T-cell anergy.
  • Signal 3 (IL-2):
    • Calcineurin inhibitors (Tac, CsA) — block IL-2 production upstream by inhibiting NFAT.
    • Basiliximab — blocks IL-2 receptor (CD25) on already-activated T-cells.
    • mTOR inhibitors (sirolimus, everolimus) — block IL-2 downstream signaling.
  • Proliferation (cell-cycle): MMF / azathioprine block lymphocyte DNA synthesis (purine pathway).
  • Generalized: Steroids — broad anti-inflammatory + lymphocyte effects.
  • B-cell / antibody: Rituximab (CD20+ B-cells), plasmapheresis (removes circulating Ab), IVIG (modulates), bortezomib (plasma cell), eculizumab (terminal complement).

The deep dive on each drug lives in the Immunosuppression tab with a shared T-cell signaling diagram that highlights where each drug blocks.

✓ Check yourself
Why does standard maintenance use THREE drugs (tacrolimus + MMF + prednisone) instead of one strong one?
Because T-cell activation requires multiple sequential signals, and a single drug blocking one signal can be overridden by the others or by drug-level dips. Hitting three different points (CNI = signal 3 production; MMF = proliferation; steroid = broad) lets each drug be used at a lower dose with fewer side effects, and provides redundancy. Same principle as combination antiretrovirals or combination chemotherapy.

✓ Check yourself
A KT patient at month 4 has trough Tac 9, MMF 1g BID, pred 5 mg daily, valganciclovir 450 mg daily, TMP-SMX SS daily. Anything unusual?
Nothing unusual — this is the standard regimen. Valganciclovir is being renal-adjusted (450 mg suggests eGFR ~40-60). Tac trough is at-target for month 4 (target 5-8 long-term; 8-12 early; 9 is just above the steady-state target — would consider dose-down slightly if stable). Watch for cumulative TMP-SMX hyperK and valganciclovir-induced leukopenia at follow-up.
The patient is admitted with sinusitis and the ENT team wants to start clarithromycin. Your response?
Don't. Clarithromycin is a strong CYP3A4 inhibitor — it will spike Tac levels within 2-3 days, risking acute nephrotoxicity. Suggest azithromycin instead (negligible CYP3A4 effect) or a non-macrolide. If the macrolide is essential, hold/reduce Tac and check a trough in 3-5 days.

§ 01

HLA basics — what the loci do

Before crossmatch and DSA make sense, the fellow needs to know what the HLA molecules are — what loci, what class, on what cells.

Core (always visible)
HLA Class I = HLA-A, B, C. Expressed on almost all nucleated cells (incl. donor kidney endothelium + tubular cells). Present endogenous peptides to CD8 T-cells.

HLA Class II = HLA-DR, DQ, DP. Expressed on APCs (dendritic cells, B-cells, macrophages, and — under inflammation — endothelium and tubular cells). Present exogenous peptides to CD4 T-cells.

For KT, the "big three" historically tracked at matching are A · B · DR (6 antigens, 0-6 mismatch). DQ is now recognized as a major driver of de novo DSA and is increasingly typed and reported.
Polymorphism — why mismatch is the default, not the exception

The HLA loci are the most polymorphic genes in the human genome. The IPD-IMGT/HLA database lists tens of thousands of alleles. A randomly selected unrelated pair almost never matches at all 6 antigens; even siblings have only a 25% chance of being a "perfect" 6/6 match (one in four chance per sibling pair, with the other haplotype possibilities 50% half-haplo, 25% zero-haplo).

For a fellow on rounds:

  • HLA-identical sibling — best graft survival, lowest immunosuppression need (some programs use minimal IS).
  • Half-haplotype sibling / parent → child / child → parent — typical living-related; expect ~3/6 mismatch.
  • Spouse / unrelated donor — usually 4-6/6 mismatch but outcomes excellent in modern IS era. Old "matching matters above all" view has weakened.
  • Random DDKT — most are 4-6/6 mismatch; allocation algorithms incorporate mismatch but rarely require 0-mismatch.
Which loci matter most for KT outcomes

Hierarchy (rough, based on de novo DSA risk + long-term graft survival data):

  • HLA-DR — historically the strongest predictor of acute rejection and graft survival. 0-DR mismatch = better outcomes.
  • HLA-DQ — the emerging villain. De novo DQ DSA is the most common late DSA and the strongest predictor of chronic active ABMR. Programs increasingly report DQ mismatches.
  • HLA-B — meaningful for both acute rejection and de novo DSA.
  • HLA-A — matters less than B or DR in modern series but still contributes.
  • HLA-C, DP — minor; usually reported but rarely decision-driving.

Two emerging concepts: eplet mismatch (HLAMatchmaker) counts specific amino-acid surface patches; T-cell epitope prediction (PIRCHE-II) models which donor peptides would be presented. Both predict de novo DSA risk better than antigen-level mismatch alone. Not yet routine in Thai practice but appearing in research protocols.

✓ Check yourself
The transplant report says "1A, 1B, 0DR mismatch" between donor and recipient. What does that mean?
Donor brings 1 HLA-A allele the recipient doesn't have, 1 HLA-B allele the recipient doesn't have, and shares both DR alleles. Total 2/6 mismatch at A/B/DR. The 0-DR mismatch is historically a favorable feature for graft survival. (Note: this counting convention shows mismatches at each donor antigen vs recipient — not all centers report it identically; some report mismatches per locus, some per allele.)
§ 02

PRA & calculated PRA (cPRA) — the "how sensitized?" number

A demo of the three-layer disclosure pattern this guide uses everywhere.

Core (always visible)
cPRA = the % of the deceased-donor pool against whom the patient has anti-HLA antibodies they'd reject. 0% = unsensitized, donor pool wide open. ≥80% = highly sensitized, very few donors compatible. Calculated from the patient's antibody panel vs. the donor population HLA frequencies.
Mechanism + how the number is built

The patient is tested for anti-HLA antibodies using solid-phase assays (Luminex single-antigen bead, SAB). Each detected antibody specificity (e.g., anti-HLA-A2, anti-HLA-DR7) is recorded with a Mean Fluorescence Intensity (MFI).

The lab then computes how often each of those HLA specificities appears in the local deceased-donor pool, using population frequency tables (in the US: the OPTN cPRA calculator; in Thailand: TTS-published frequencies). The output is cPRA = the fraction of donors against whom at least one of the patient's antibodies would react.

Why cPRA, not just "PRA"? Old PRA was based on actually crossmatching the patient against a panel of test cells (~10-60 cells) — limited and lab-dependent. cPRA is computed from antibody specificity + population HLA frequency, so it's reproducible and population-standardized.

Sensitization events: transfusion, pregnancy, prior transplant. Avoid transfusing the wait-listed patient when possible.

Numbers the fellow should quote
  • cPRA 0% — unsensitized, fastest path to transplant.
  • cPRA 1-19% — mildly sensitized, modest reduction in compatible pool.
  • cPRA 20-79% — moderately sensitized.
  • cPRA ≥80% — highly sensitized; UNOS/KDIGO frameworks give extra waitlist priority. cPRA 98-100% may need paired exchange or desensitization.

MFI thresholds for clinical action vary by lab; common cutoffs: MFI >1,000 = positive bead, MFI >3,000 = clinically meaningful in many programs, MFI >10,000 = strong DSA, avoid donor.

🇹🇭

The Thai Red Cross Organ Donation Centre maintains the national waitlist with cPRA-based priority similar to UNOS. TTS publishes locally derived HLA allele frequencies for cPRA calculation; using US frequencies overestimates cPRA in Thai patients. ✓ Check yourself
Your patient has a cPRA of 95% with an anti-HLA-A2 antibody at MFI 8,500. What does this mean practically?
Highly sensitized — only ~5% of deceased donors would be acceptable. The anti-A2 at MFI 8,500 is a clinically meaningful DSA against any A2+ donor; that donor would be avoided unless desensitization is planned. Options: (1) wait on the list with priority bonus, (2) paired exchange with a willing living donor, (3) desensitization protocol (IVIG ± rituximab ± PLEX).
§ 03

Donor-specific antibodies & MFI interpretation

DSA = anti-HLA antibody whose target is on the donor. Distinct from generic anti-HLA antibodies (which contribute to cPRA but only matter for a specific donor if that donor carries the target).

Core (always visible)
DSA is detected by solid-phase Luminex single-antigen beads (SAB). Each bead carries one HLA antigen; the assay reports a Mean Fluorescence Intensity (MFI) for each antibody specificity. MFI is semi-quantitative — useful for trends, comparison-sensitive across labs, not a true antibody titre.

Common practical cutoffs (lab-dependent — confirm with your HLA lab):
MFI > 1,000 = bead positive · > 3,000 = clinically meaningful in many programs · > 10,000 = strong DSA, avoid the donor without desensitization. These are illustrative — Tambur et al. (AJT 2018, Sensitization in Transplantation consensus) discourages cross-lab MFI thresholds; always confirm with your HLA lab.
Class I vs Class II DSA — different risks

Class I DSA (anti-A, -B, -C) — targets antigens on endothelium and most graft cells. Historically associated with hyperacute rejection if present pre-transplant; now caught by virtual crossmatch. Pre-formed Class I DSA is the classic sensitized-patient scenario.

Class II DSA (anti-DR, -DQ, -DP) — targets antigens primarily on APCs and inflamed endothelium. De novo DQ DSA is now recognized as the dominant driver of chronic active ABMR and late graft loss. Risk factors for de novo DSA: non-adherence, sub-therapeutic IS levels, prior rejection, DR/DQ mismatch.

Practical: a stable patient who develops de novo Class II DSA at year 3 is a red flag — check tacrolimus trough, check medication adherence, consider protocol biopsy, monitor MFI.

Complement-binding DSA — C1q / C3d

Some DSAs activate complement (C1q+), some don't. C1q+ DSA is more strongly associated with active ABMR, severe histology, and worse outcomes. The standard SAB only tells you the antibody is there; supplementary C1q assays (or C3d) report whether it's complement-fixing.

Caveat: high-MFI Class I DSA is almost always C1q+ regardless of which test is used — at high titre the strength alone predicts complement activation. C1q testing adds the most information for moderate-MFI antibodies (3000-10000) where the clinical significance is uncertain.

De novo DSA — what triggers monitoring

KDIGO 2009 suggests DSA testing at minimum at the time of acute rejection or unexplained graft dysfunction. Many programs now do protocol DSA screening: every 3 months in the first year, then annually if negative. High-risk recipients (sensitized, repeat tx, prior rejection) get more frequent monitoring.

When de novo DSA appears:

  • Confirm with a repeat test 2-4 weeks later (rule out lab noise).
  • Check tacrolimus trough and adherence.
  • Consider protocol biopsy to look for subclinical ABMR (g + ptc).
  • If active ABMR: treat. If sub-clinical with normal histology: optimize IS + monitor.
🇹🇭
Thai practice
DSA testing availability is concentrated in major Thai centers (Siriraj, Ramathibodi, Chulalongkorn, KCMH). Cost is a real constraint — protocol monitoring intensity often less aggressive than US/EU programs. SAB Luminex is the standard where available; C1q is research-grade in most Thai centers.
✓ Check yourself
At month 18 post-tx, your stable patient develops a new DQ7 antibody at MFI 5,500. Donor was DQ7+. Cr is 1.2 (stable). What now?
De novo Class II DSA against the donor — clinically meaningful (above the 3,000 threshold) but not yet causing dysfunction. Steps: (1) confirm with repeat in 2-4 weeks, (2) check Tac trough (often sub-therapeutic in this scenario — patient skipping doses or pharmacy gap), (3) ask about adherence directly, (4) consider protocol biopsy to rule out subclinical ABMR (g/ptc). If biopsy active ABMR: treat. If quiet: optimize IS to ensure adequate exposure, monitor MFI every 1-3 months.

Core (always visible) — what each test actually detects
CDC (Complement-Dependent Cytotoxicity) — the original 1960s test. Recipient serum + donor lymphocytes + complement → if recipient has high-titre complement-fixing DSA, donor cells lyse. Cheap, low sensitivity, can be confounded by autoantibodies or rituximab.

FCXM (Flow Crossmatch) — recipient serum + donor cells + fluorescent secondary antibody → flow cytometry detects bound IgG. ~10× more sensitive than CDC. Doesn't require complement fixation.

Virtual Crossmatch (VXM) — compares the recipient's Luminex SAB antibody profile (specificities + MFI) against the typed donor HLA. No donor cells needed. Used to pre-screen waitlist offers; complements (not replaces) cellular crossmatch.
False positives + when each test misleads
  • CDC false-positive — IgM autoantibodies (treat with DTT to reduce), rituximab (binds CD20 → kills donor B cells in the test). Distinguish T-cell vs B-cell CDC.
  • FCXM false-positive — IVIG (recently administered), high background autoantibodies, rituximab on B-cell flow.
  • Virtual XM false-negative — antibodies against antigens not on the donor; antibodies present at low MFI; rare HLA alleles not on the SAB panel.
  • Virtual XM false-positive — denatured HLA on beads can detect antibodies that wouldn't bind native HLA on real cells (the "denatured DSA" problem; especially Class I single-antigen beads).
When to use which — modern practice

Pre-listing: SAB Luminex defines the antibody profile → cPRA → unacceptable antigen list. VXM done against every offered donor.

Pre-transplant final check: CDC + FCXM against actual donor cells (cellular crossmatch). KDIGO 2009 recommends a final pre-transplant crossmatch; TTC 2 §17 specifies a 2-week pre-op window.

Highly sensitized patient: Some programs are now moving to virtual-crossmatch-only protocols when the SAB profile is well-characterized and the donor HLA is known — saves time, allows long-distance allocation. Still uncommon in Thailand outside major centers.

Thai practice (TTC 2 §16-17)
The TTC framework requires early ABO + HLA sensitization triage within 2-4 weeks of donor referral to avoid wasting evaluation cost on incompatible pairs. The "final crossmatch" must be performed within 2 weeks before surgery (TTC 2 §17) — any blood transfusion or sensitizing event in the interim mandates a repeat.
✓ Check yourself
Try this in the widget above: pick "Sensitized recipient" scenario. Why is the virtual XM positive but you might still hesitate to call CDC positive?
VXM is positive because the recipient has anti-B44 at MFI 12,000 and the donor is B44+ — that's a clear DSA. CDC at 12,000 MFI for a Class I antibody is likely positive (high-titre Class I usually fixes complement), but CDC sensitivity is lower than flow — a borderline-strength antibody might miss CDC and only show on FCXM. In practice you'd treat the patient as immunologically positive (high-MFI Class I DSA) regardless of which test reads "positive" — the widget defaults reflect the most likely real-world readings, not a guarantee.
Patient on rituximab 2 weeks ago has a positive B-cell CDC against the donor. Real or artifact?
Likely artifact. Rituximab kills the donor B cells in the CDC test, mimicking complement-mediated lysis → false-positive B-cell CDC. Distinguish by: (1) checking T-cell CDC (should be negative if it's just the rituximab artifact), (2) repeating with rituximab-pretreated cells or extra washes, (3) cross-referencing with FCXM and VXM. Don't refuse the donor on B-cell CDC alone if SAB shows no DSA.
§ 05

ABO-incompatible & paired exchange

ABO compatibility is the other immunologic gate — separate from HLA. The natural anti-A / anti-B isohaemagglutinins are pre-formed and would cause hyperacute rejection of an ABO-incompatible graft without intervention.

Core (always visible)
The ABO blood group A and B antigens are expressed on the donor's endothelium. A recipient with anti-A (group B or O) receiving a Group A kidney would have hyperacute rejection within minutes to hours of revascularization — classically as the surgeon releases the vascular clamps. Mechanism: IgM/IgG natural antibodies + complement → endothelial necrosis + thrombosis.

Two ways around an ABO-incompatible willing donor:
① ABO-i transplant with desensitization — lower the recipient's anti-A/B titre to a center-defined threshold (commonly ≤1:8 IgG per Japanese/Stockholm protocols, ≤1:16 IgG historically at Hopkins) with PLEX + IVIG ± rituximab pre-op, then transplant. Outcomes approach ABO-compatible in experienced centers.
② Paired Kidney Exchange (PKE) — swap with another incompatible pair so both recipients get an ABO-compatible donor. No desensitization burden.
ABO compatibility matrix + the "universal donor" point

Same as transfusion compatibility (not Rh-relevant for kidneys — RhD is on RBCs, not endothelium):

  • O recipient → can only receive from O donor (anti-A and anti-B).
  • A recipient → from A or O.
  • B recipient → from B or O.
  • AB recipient → from any (no natural anti-A/B).

Group O recipients face the longest waits in deceased-donor allocation because they can only take O kidneys but O kidneys can go to anyone — net flow goes away from group O. Some allocation algorithms partly correct for this.

Paired kidney exchange — how the chain works

Simplest case: 2-way swap. Pair 1 (donor type A, recipient type B) and Pair 2 (donor type B, recipient type A) swap donors so each recipient gets a compatible organ. Surgery for both pairs is scheduled the same day to prevent withdrawal.

Chains: An altruistic (non-directed) donor triggers a chain — gives to a sensitized/incompatible pair's recipient; that pair's donor gives to another pair; etc. Chains can be very long (the longest published >30 transplants).

In Thailand: the Medical Council restricts donation to family members or 3+ year spouses, which limits non-directed altruistic donation. PKE within family-related pairs is technically possible but uncommon.

🇹🇭
Thai practice
ABO-incompatible KT and paired exchange are concentrated in major Thai centers (Siriraj, Ramathibodi, Chulalongkorn) and remain uncommon nationally. Most ABO-incompatible willing pairs in Thailand are either declined or referred for desensitization at a high-volume center; PKE programs exist but are small. Specific TTC 2/4 protocols on titre thresholds and desensitization regimens to be verified verbatim in Ship #4 critic.
✓ Check yourself
Group O recipient with a willing Group A spouse-donor. Three pathways forward?
(1) ABO-incompatible KT with desensitization — PLEX + IVIG ± rituximab to lower the recipient's anti-A titre below the center threshold (~1:8 IgG), then transplant. Lifelong augmented IS not required after the early accommodation period in most series. (2) Paired exchange — register the pair with the national PKE program if one exists; swap with another incompatible pair. (3) Wait for a deceased-donor offer (group O only, longest wait). The choice depends on center expertise, recipient sensitization, and family flexibility.
§ 06

Desensitization — IVIG, rituximab, PLEX

For the sensitized patient (high cPRA, DSA against the only available donor) or the ABO-incompatible pair, desensitization lowers the antibody barrier enough to transplant. Burden is real; outcomes are experienced-center-dependent.

Core (always visible)
Three mainstays, used in combination:
① Plasmapheresis (PLEX) — physically removes circulating antibody. Each session ~1-1.5 plasma volume, replacement with FFP or albumin. Typically 5-10 sessions pre-op + on-demand post-op.
② IVIG — modulates immunity (saturates Fc receptors, neutralizes circulating antibody, anti-idiotypic effects). Dosed 1-2 g/kg over 1-2 days, often paired with PLEX (a "PLEX → IVIG sandwich" each cycle).
③ Rituximab — anti-CD20, depletes B-cells, reduces ongoing antibody production. Single dose 375 mg/m² (or fixed 500 mg) pre-op in many protocols.

Bortezomib (proteasome inhibitor, hits plasma cells) and eculizumab (terminal complement) are reserved for refractory cases.
When to desensitize vs paired-exchange

Desensitization is resource- and risk-heavy. Major centers prefer paired exchange first when feasible, reserving desensitization for:

  • Highly sensitized recipients (cPRA >95%) with no realistic PKE match.
  • ABO-incompatible pairs where no swap is available.
  • Center-specific protocols favoring desensitization (varies by program).

Outcomes: in experienced US/EU centers, desensitized HLA-incompatible KT has graft and patient survival advantage over remaining on the waitlist (Orandi et al, NEJM 2016;374:940), though acute ABMR rates (~15-20%) and chronic active ABMR rates remain elevated vs. standard HLA-compatible KT (<5%). ABO-incompatible KT outcomes are generally better than HLA-incompatible — the ABO barrier is more "accommodatable" than the HLA barrier.

Practical regimen — typical sequence
  1. Week -4 to -2: Rituximab 375 mg/m² once. Confirms B-cell depletion (CD19/CD20 count).
  2. Week -2 to OR: PLEX every other day, 5-10 sessions, with IVIG 100 mg/kg after each PLEX. Larger IVIG dose (1-2 g/kg) on the last day if regimen calls for it. Monitor DSA MFI or anti-A/B titre weekly.
  3. OR day: Confirm crossmatch / titre below threshold. Standard induction (basiliximab or rATG).
  4. Post-op: Standard maintenance (Tac + MMF + steroid). PLEX on demand if early ABMR markers (Cr rise, DSA rebound, biopsy g/ptc/C4d).

Splenectomy (largely historical) and bortezomib + carfilzomib are 2nd-line in refractory cases.

✓ Check yourself
Sensitized recipient with anti-B44 MFI 11,000; donor is B44+. Pre-op regimen?
Likely rituximab 375 mg/m² ×1 (week -3 or -4), then 5-7 sessions of PLEX every other day in the 2 weeks before surgery, each followed by IVIG 100 mg/kg; a final IVIG 1-2 g/kg may be given on the last day. Goal: pre-op FCXM negative or anti-B44 MFI <3,000. Induction with rATG (high-immunologic-risk regimen). Post-op DSA monitoring weekly with PLEX on rebound. Refer to a high-volume center if your program isn't routinely doing this.

§ 03

Calcineurin inhibitors — Tacrolimus & Cyclosporine

A demo of the drug-card pattern. Other drugs follow this same shape in Ship #4.

Tacrolimus — Core (always visible)
The dominant calcineurin inhibitor (CNI) in modern KT. Blocks T-cell activation. Trough goal 8-12 ng/mL months 0-3, then 5-8 ng/mL long-term. Main toxicities: nephrotoxicity (acute vasoconstrictive + chronic IFTA), NODAT, tremor, hyperkalaemia, hypomagnesaemia. CYP3A4/P-gp substrate — interaction-heavy.
Mechanism (with the specific molecular target)

Tac binds FKBP12 intracellularly. The Tac-FKBP12 complex inhibits calcineurin, the phosphatase that dephosphorylates NFAT (Nuclear Factor of Activated T-cells). With calcineurin blocked, NFAT stays phosphorylated and can't enter the nucleus → no IL-2 (and other cytokines) transcription → T-cell activation is blunted.

CsA does the same thing via a different binding partner (cyclophilin instead of FKBP12). Same downstream effect, similar toxicities, different drug-interaction profile.

Dosing, trough targets, and the level/dose math

Starting dose: ~0.1-0.15 mg/kg/day divided BID. First trough at day 3-5.

Trough targets by month:

  • Month 0-3: 8-12 ng/mL (some programs 10-15 in first weeks)
  • Month 3-6: 6-10 ng/mL
  • Month 6+: 5-8 ng/mL (sometimes as low as 4-6 in stable patients with low immunologic risk)

Trough is drawn just before the next dose (12 h after last dose, BID dosing). Steady state reached in ~3 days at a given dose. Once-daily Tac (Advagraf/Envarsus) has different troughs — confirm with the prescribing program.

Adjustment heuristic: Tac level changes roughly proportionally to dose at steady state. If trough is 14 and target is 8, drop dose by ~40%. Recheck in 3-5 days.

Side effects — what to look for
  • Nephrotoxicity — acute (vasoconstrictive: rising Cr at high levels, reversible with dose ↓) and chronic (IFTA, irreversible at the cell level)
  • NODAT — Tac is more diabetogenic than CsA. Cumulative incidence 10-30% by year 1. Screen weekly ×4, then taper.
  • Neurotoxicity — tremor (very common, dose-related), headache, insomnia, rarely PRES (posterior reversible encephalopathy)
  • Electrolytes — hyperkalaemia (Type 4 RTA-like), hypomagnesaemia (repleting Mg sometimes helps the tremor)
  • HUS/TMA — Tac-induced TMA can present as Cr rise + falling platelets + schistocytes; biopsy distinguishes from rejection
  • Alopecia in women (CsA causes hirsutism — opposite!)
  • Hypertension — less than CsA but still common
Drug interactions you must know

Tac is a CYP3A4 and P-glycoprotein substrate. Anything that inhibits or induces CYP3A4 changes the level dramatically — sometimes 5-10× within days.

↑↑ Tac level (use with caution + watch troughs):

  • Azoles (fluconazole, itraconazole, voriconazole, posaconazole) — major
  • Diltiazem, verapamil
  • Macrolides (erythromycin, clarithromycin — not azithromycin)
  • Protease inhibitors (HIV, HCV)
  • Grapefruit juice

↓↓ Tac level (risk: rejection):

  • Rifampin (massive inducer)
  • Phenytoin, carbamazepine, phenobarbital
  • St. John's wort

Practical rule: any new medicine for a KT patient on Tac → check the interaction. If it interacts, get a trough 3-5 days after starting.

✓ Check yourself
Your KT patient's Tac trough jumped from 8 to 22 ng/mL last week. What did they probably get prescribed?
A CYP3A4 inhibitor — most commonly fluconazole for thrush/yeast, diltiazem for BP, or a macrolide for sinusitis/pneumonia. Grapefruit juice or pomelo are easy to miss too. Hold Tac for 1-2 doses, restart at ~50%, recheck trough in 3-5 days.
Patient on Tac develops a fine hand tremor that wasn't there before. What's the workup?
Common Tac side effect — first check the trough. If supratherapeutic, dose down. If on target, check Mg (low Mg worsens Tac tremor); replete. If persistent + disabling, consider Tac → CsA switch (CsA also tremor but often better tolerated). Severe acute neurotoxicity (confusion, seizures, vision loss) → think PRES, get MRI, stop Tac.

§ 02

TCMR vs ABMR — the one comparison the fellow must own

Skeleton-level worked example. Full Banff explorer + lesion-by-lesion detail authored in Ship #5.

Core (always visible)
TCMR = T-cell mediated. Driven by recipient T-cells attacking the graft. Histology: tubulitis (t-score), interstitial inflammation (i-score), ± intimal arteritis (v-score). Treat: steroids → rATG.

ABMR = antibody-mediated. Driven by donor-specific antibodies (DSA). Histology: microvascular inflammation (g + ptc), C4d staining, DSA positive. Treat: PLEX + IVIG + rituximab.

Mixed rejection has both. Treat both pathways.
Banff 2022 categories at a glance (verbatim criteria in Ship #5)

Active TCMR grading:

  • Ia — i > threshold + t2 (moderate tubulitis)
  • Ib — i > threshold + t3 (severe tubulitis)
  • IIa — mild-to-moderate intimal arteritis (v1)
  • IIb — severe intimal arteritis (v2)
  • III — transmural arteritis, fibrinoid necrosis (v3)
  • Borderline — tubulitis without sufficient inflammation for Ia

Active ABMR — requires all three: (1) histologic features (g + ptc + ± C4d), (2) DSA, (3) MVI threshold met. Exact thresholds quoted in Ship #5.

Chronic active ABMR — adds cg (transplant glomerulopathy) on top of active features.

Banff 2022 new emphasis: microvascular inflammation reappraisal — MVI without DSA is now its own category (DSA-negative MVI).

✓ Check yourself
Day +90, Cr rises from 1.1 → 1.7. Biopsy shows g2 + ptc2 + C4d-positive PTCs. Latest DSA panel is positive for anti-HLA-DR4 MFI 6,500. Diagnosis and treatment?
Active antibody-mediated rejection — meets the three criteria (MVI = g + ptc, C4d+, DSA+). Treatment: PLEX + IVIG + rituximab. Monitor DSA, repeat biopsy at week 2-4 if no Cr response. Ensure CNI exposure adequate (not under-immunosuppressed). Long-term: this patient is at high risk for chronic active ABMR / transplant glomerulopathy — monitor DSA persistently.
