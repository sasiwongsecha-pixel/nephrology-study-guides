✓ Check yourself
Day +30, patient is febrile with Cr rise from 1.2 → 1.8 and leukopenia. The two diagnoses you must consider first?
(1) CMV viraemia/disease — leukopenia + fever in months 1-6 is CMV until proven otherwise. Send PCR. (2) Acute rejection (TCMR) — Cr rise in the first months. Biopsy if PCR negative or if rejection is the more likely diagnosis based on IS levels.
You see a 6-month post-tx patient with Cr 1.5 (baseline 1.0) and BK PCR 250,000 copies/mL. First intervention?
Reduce immunosuppression — drop the antimetabolite (MMF/AZA) first by ~50%, then lower the CNI trough target. There is no effective antiviral for BK. Biopsy if Cr doesn't recover or to confirm BKVAN (SV40+ inclusions). Don't add cidofovir/leflunomide first-line.

§ 02

PRA & calculated PRA (cPRA) — the "how sensitized?" number

A demo of the three-layer disclosure pattern this guide uses everywhere.

Core (always visible)
cPRA = the % of the deceased-donor pool against whom the patient has anti-HLA antibodies they'd reject. 0% = unsensitized, donor pool wide open. ≥80% = highly sensitized, very few donors compatible. Calculated from the patient's antibody panel vs. the donor population HLA frequencies.
Mechanism + how the number is built

The patient is tested for anti-HLA antibodies using solid-phase assays (Luminex single-antigen bead, SAB). Each detected antibody specificity (e.g., anti-HLA-A2, anti-HLA-DR7) is recorded with a Mean Fluorescence Intensity (MFI).

The lab then computes how often each of those HLA specificities appears in the local deceased-donor pool, using population frequency tables (in the US: the OPTN cPRA calculator; in Thailand: TTS-published frequencies). The output is cPRA = the fraction of donors against whom at least one of the patient's antibodies would react.

Why cPRA, not just "PRA"? Old PRA was based on actually crossmatching the patient against a panel of test cells (~10-60 cells) — limited and lab-dependent. cPRA is computed from antibody specificity + population HLA frequency, so it's reproducible and population-standardized.

Sensitization events: transfusion, pregnancy, prior transplant. Avoid transfusing the wait-listed patient when possible.

Numbers the fellow should quote
  • cPRA 0% — unsensitized, fastest path to transplant.
  • cPRA 1-19% — mildly sensitized, modest reduction in compatible pool.
  • cPRA 20-79% — moderately sensitized.
  • cPRA ≥80% — highly sensitized; UNOS/KDIGO frameworks give extra waitlist priority. cPRA 98-100% may need paired exchange or desensitization.

MFI thresholds for clinical action vary by lab; common cutoffs: MFI >1,000 = positive bead, MFI >3,000 = clinically meaningful in many programs, MFI >10,000 = strong DSA, avoid donor.

✓ Check yourself
Your patient has a cPRA of 95% with an anti-HLA-A2 antibody at MFI 8,500. What does this mean practically?
Highly sensitized — only ~5% of deceased donors would be acceptable. The anti-A2 at MFI 8,500 is a clinically meaningful DSA against any A2+ donor; that donor would be avoided unless desensitization is planned. Options: (1) wait on the list with priority bonus, (2) paired exchange with a willing living donor, (3) desensitization protocol (IVIG ± rituximab ± PLEX).

§ 03

Calcineurin inhibitors — Tacrolimus & Cyclosporine

A demo of the drug-card pattern. Other drugs follow this same shape in Ship #4.

Tacrolimus — Core (always visible)
The dominant calcineurin inhibitor (CNI) in modern KT. Blocks T-cell activation. Trough goal 8-12 ng/mL months 0-3, then 5-8 ng/mL long-term. Main toxicities: nephrotoxicity (acute vasoconstrictive + chronic IFTA), NODAT, tremor, hyperkalaemia, hypomagnesaemia. CYP3A4/P-gp substrate — interaction-heavy.
Mechanism (with the specific molecular target)

Tac binds FKBP12 intracellularly. The Tac-FKBP12 complex inhibits calcineurin, the phosphatase that dephosphorylates NFAT (Nuclear Factor of Activated T-cells). With calcineurin blocked, NFAT stays phosphorylated and can't enter the nucleus → no IL-2 (and other cytokines) transcription → T-cell activation is blunted.

CsA does the same thing via a different binding partner (cyclophilin instead of FKBP12). Same downstream effect, similar toxicities, different drug-interaction profile.

Dosing, trough targets, and the level/dose math

Starting dose: ~0.1-0.15 mg/kg/day divided BID. First trough at day 3-5.

Trough targets by month:

  • Month 0-3: 8-12 ng/mL (some programs 10-15 in first weeks)
  • Month 3-6: 6-10 ng/mL
  • Month 6+: 5-8 ng/mL (sometimes as low as 4-6 in stable patients with low immunologic risk)

Trough is drawn just before the next dose (12 h after last dose, BID dosing). Steady state reached in ~3 days at a given dose. Once-daily Tac (Advagraf/Envarsus) has different troughs — confirm with the prescribing program.

Adjustment heuristic: Tac level changes roughly proportionally to dose at steady state. If trough is 14 and target is 8, drop dose by ~40%. Recheck in 3-5 days.

Side effects — what to look for
  • Nephrotoxicity — acute (vasoconstrictive: rising Cr at high levels, reversible with dose ↓) and chronic (IFTA, irreversible at the cell level)
  • NODAT — Tac is more diabetogenic than CsA. Cumulative incidence 10-30% by year 1. Screen weekly ×4, then taper.
  • Neurotoxicity — tremor (very common, dose-related), headache, insomnia, rarely PRES (posterior reversible encephalopathy)
  • Electrolytes — hyperkalaemia (Type 4 RTA-like), hypomagnesaemia (repleting Mg sometimes helps the tremor)
  • HUS/TMA — Tac-induced TMA can present as Cr rise + falling platelets + schistocytes; biopsy distinguishes from rejection
  • Alopecia in women (CsA causes hirsutism — opposite!)
  • Hypertension — less than CsA but still common
Drug interactions you must know

Tac is a CYP3A4 and P-glycoprotein substrate. Anything that inhibits or induces CYP3A4 changes the level dramatically — sometimes 5-10× within days.

↑↑ Tac level (use with caution + watch troughs):

  • Azoles (fluconazole, itraconazole, voriconazole, posaconazole) — major
  • Diltiazem, verapamil
  • Macrolides (erythromycin, clarithromycin — not azithromycin)
  • Protease inhibitors (HIV, HCV)
  • Grapefruit juice

↓↓ Tac level (risk: rejection):

  • Rifampin (massive inducer)
  • Phenytoin, carbamazepine, phenobarbital
  • St. John's wort

Practical rule: any new medicine for a KT patient on Tac → check the interaction. If it interacts, get a trough 3-5 days after starting.

✓ Check yourself
Your KT patient's Tac trough jumped from 8 to 22 ng/mL last week. What did they probably get prescribed?
A CYP3A4 inhibitor — most commonly fluconazole for thrush/yeast, diltiazem for BP, or a macrolide for sinusitis/pneumonia. Grapefruit juice or pomelo are easy to miss too. Hold Tac for 1-2 doses, restart at ~50%, recheck trough in 3-5 days.
Patient on Tac develops a fine hand tremor that wasn't there before. What's the workup?
Common Tac side effect — first check the trough. If supratherapeutic, dose down. If on target, check Mg (low Mg worsens Tac tremor); replete. If persistent + disabling, consider Tac → CsA switch (CsA also tremor but often better tolerated). Severe acute neurotoxicity (confusion, seizures, vision loss) → think PRES, get MRI, stop Tac.

§ 02

TCMR vs ABMR — the one comparison the fellow must own

Skeleton-level worked example. Full Banff explorer + lesion-by-lesion detail authored in Ship #5.

Core (always visible)
TCMR = T-cell mediated. Driven by recipient T-cells attacking the graft. Histology: tubulitis (t-score), interstitial inflammation (i-score), ± intimal arteritis (v-score). Treat: steroids → rATG.

ABMR = antibody-mediated. Driven by donor-specific antibodies (DSA). Histology: microvascular inflammation (g + ptc), C4d staining, DSA positive. Treat: PLEX + IVIG + rituximab.

Mixed rejection has both. Treat both pathways.
Banff 2022 categories at a glance (verbatim criteria in Ship #5)

Active TCMR grading:

  • Ia — i > threshold + t2 (moderate tubulitis)
  • Ib — i > threshold + t3 (severe tubulitis)
  • IIa — mild-to-moderate intimal arteritis (v1)
  • IIb — severe intimal arteritis (v2)
  • III — transmural arteritis, fibrinoid necrosis (v3)
  • Borderline — tubulitis without sufficient inflammation for Ia

Active ABMR — requires all three: (1) histologic features (g + ptc + ± C4d), (2) DSA, (3) MVI threshold met. Exact thresholds quoted in Ship #5.

Chronic active ABMR — adds cg (transplant glomerulopathy) on top of active features.

Banff 2022 new emphasis: microvascular inflammation reappraisal — MVI without DSA is now its own category (DSA-negative MVI).

✓ Check yourself
Day +90, Cr rises from 1.1 → 1.7. Biopsy shows g2 + ptc2 + C4d-positive PTCs. Latest DSA panel is positive for anti-HLA-DR4 MFI 6,500. Diagnosis and treatment?
Active antibody-mediated rejection — meets the three criteria (MVI = g + ptc, C4d+, DSA+). Treatment: PLEX + IVIG + rituximab. Monitor DSA, repeat biopsy at week 2-4 if no Cr response. Ensure CNI exposure adequate (not under-immunosuppressed). Long-term: this patient is at high risk for chronic active ABMR / transplant glomerulopathy — monitor DSA persistently.
