# Clinical and Regulatory Frameworks for Kidney Transplantation: Living Donor Evaluation and Immunological Verification

The provided source material, primarily comprising the **Thai Transplant Care (TTC 2)** guidelines (September 2016) and references to the **United Kingdom Guidelines for Living Donor Kidney Transplantation (2011)**, establishes a rigorous framework for the evaluation of living kidney donors. This briefing document synthesizes the legal, ethical, and clinical protocols necessary for a nephrology fellow to manage the transplant candidate and donor evaluation process, with a focus on immunological verification and physiological suitability.

## Executive Summary

The evaluation of a living kidney donor is governed by strict ethical mandates to ensure the donor's safety and the legality of the organ gift. Central to the Thai clinical practice is the **Medical Council of Thailand’s Regulation (2010)**, which defines permissible relationships for donation and mandates the use of **HLA and DNA testing** as biological proof of genetic kinship when legal documentation is insufficient. Clinical suitability is determined through a multi-tiered assessment of the Glomerular Filtration Rate (GFR), with a preference for the **CKD-EPI (2009)** equation for initial screening and measured GFR (mGFR) or individual kidney GFR for complex cases. Stringent thresholds for proteinuria (AER < 100 mg/day) and microscopic hematuria are utilized to exclude donors with latent renal pathology.

## 1. Regulatory and Ethical Governance

The legal framework for living kidney donation (LD) is designed to prevent organ trafficking and ensure informed consent.

### 1.1 Permissible Donor-Recipient Relationships
According to the Medical Council’s regulations and Thai Red Cross Society protocols, a living donor must belong to one of the following categories:
*   **Genetic Kinship:** Parents, children, or siblings. If the relationship is in doubt, DNA and HLA testing are required for verification [TTC 2, Page 8].
*   **Legal/De Facto Spouse:** Must be legally married for at least three years. If there is a common child, the three-year requirement is waived, but DNA testing may be required to prove paternity/maternity [TTC 2, Page 11].
*   **Foreign Nationals:** For cases where the donor, recipient, or both are foreigners, documentation must be certified by the relevant embassy and the Ministry of Foreign Affairs. Genetic relationships must be confirmed via DNA and HLA testing performed by a Thai state medical institute [TTC 2, Page 14].

### 1.2 Ethical Mandates
The "Thai Transplant Care" document outlines that the transplant surgeon must be a certified specialist recognized by the Medical Council and that the procedure must occur in a hospital that is a member of the Thai Red Cross Organ Donation Centre [TTC 2, Page 8]. Donors have the absolute right to withdraw consent at any time prior to the surgery [TTC 2, Page 11].

## 2. Immunological and Genetic Verification

While the source focuses on the evaluation process, it identifies HLA and crossmatch as critical gatekeepers in the transplant timeline.

### 2.1 The Role of HLA in Relationship Verification
HLA (Human Leukocyte Antigen) is used primarily as a scientific tool to confirm biological relationships in the Thai regulatory context.
*   **Sibling/Parental Proof:** HLA testing is mandated when legal documents cannot sufficiently prove a first-degree or second-degree (aunts, uncles, cousins) genetic relationship [TTC 2, Page 11, 14].
*   **Spousal Proof:** In instances where a marriage certificate is not present but a common child exists, HLA and DNA testing of the child and parents serve as the primary evidence of the relationship [TTC 2, Page 13].

### 2.2 Crossmatch and Sensitization
The protocol incorporates the UK 2011 guidelines for managing sensitization:
*   **Initial Triage:** Early identification of potential ABO incompatibility or HLA sensitization is required within 2–4 weeks of donor referral to avoid unnecessary investigations of unsuitable pairs [TTC 2, Page 16].
*   **Final Crossmatch:** A "Final Crossmatch" must be performed within the **two weeks prior to the transplant** to ensure no new de novo donor-specific antibodies (DSA) have developed and to confirm the safety of the procedure [TTC 2, Page 17].

## 3. Quantitative Assessment of Renal Reserve (GFR)

The assessment of the donor's kidney function is the cornerstone of long-term post-donation safety.

### 3.1 GFR Measurement Protocols
*   **Primary Screening:** The **CKD-EPI 2009** equation (eGFRcr) is recommended for initial assessment. All results must be indexed to Body Surface Area (mL/min/1.73 m²) [TTC 2, Page 26].
*   **Verification (mGFR):** If eGFR is borderline or accuracy is questioned, measured GFR (mGFR) using exogenous markers (e.g., Inulin, Iothalamate, 51Cr-EDTA, Iohexol, or 99mTc-DTPA) is the gold standard [TTC 2, Page 26].
*   **Individual Kidney Function:** If a size discrepancy (>10% difference) or anatomical abnormality is noted via ultrasound or CT, radionuclides (e.g., 99mTc-DTPA) must be used to measure the GFR of each kidney separately. The surgeon should harvest the kidney with the lower function, leaving the donor with the more robust organ [TTC 2, Page 27].

### 3.2 Acceptance Thresholds
| GFR Level (mL/min/1.73 m²) | Action |
| :--- | :--- |
| **> 90** | Acceptable for donation. |
| **70 – 89** | May be acceptable after rigorous risk counseling regarding age, comorbidities, and the risk of future decline. |
| **< 70** | Should be excluded from living donation. |

*Source: TTC 2, Page 27.*

## 4. Systematic Screening and Risk Stratification

Donors undergo exhaustive screening to rule out latent systemic or renal disease.

### 4.1 Proteinuria and Albuminuria
The guidelines prioritize Albumin-to-Creatinine Ratio (ACR) over total protein measurements.
*   **Exclusion Criteria:** Donors with an Albumin Excretion Rate (AER) **> 100 mg/day** or total protein excretion **> 300 mg/day** (from a 24-hour collection) are excluded [TTC 2, Page 29].

### 4.2 Hematuria
Microscopic hematuria is defined as **3–5 RBCs per high power field** in centrifuged urine.
*   **Persistent Hematuria:** If found in two or more samples, it requires a full workup, including urine culture (to rule out UTI), ultrasound (to rule out stones or malignancy), and potentially a kidney biopsy if a glomerular cause is suspected [TTC 2, Page 31].

### 4.3 UK Guidelines for Routine Donor Screening
The TTC 2 adopts the UK 2011 screening list, which includes:
*   **Virology:** Hepatitis B/C, HIV, HTLV 1/2, CMV, EBV, Syphilis, and Varicella.
*   **Cardiovascular:** ECG is routine; Echo and stress tests are indicated for those with risk factors or older age [TTC 2, Pages 21–22].

## Actionable Insights for the Nephrology Fellow

1.  **Early Triage of Donor Pairs:** Within the first 2–4 weeks of referral, prioritize ABO and HLA compatibility checks. Do not proceed to expensive imaging (CT angiography) until immunological feasibility is established.
2.  **Verify Relationships Early:** For non-traditional pairings or foreign nationals, initiate the HLA/DNA verification process immediately, as this requires coordination with state medical institutes and embassies.
3.  **Strict GFR Interpretation:** Do not rely solely on serum creatinine. Use the CKD-EPI 2009 equation for all donors. If the donor is older or has a borderline eGFR (70–89), proceed to a 24-hour creatinine clearance (average of two collections) or a nuclear mGFR.
4.  **Manage Size Mismatches:** If imaging shows one kidney is significantly smaller, ensure a nuclear scan (99mTc-DTPA) is performed. The policy is to leave the "better" kidney with the donor to maximize their long-term safety.
5.  **Final Clearance:** Ensure the "Final Crossmatch" is scheduled exactly within the 14-day window before the operation. Any intervening blood transfusions or sensitizing events during the workup period necessitate a repeat of this test.
