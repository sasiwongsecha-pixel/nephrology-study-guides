# Thai Transplantation Care (TTC) 2: Kidney Transplantation Study Guide

This study guide is synthesized from the **Thai Transplantation Care (TTC) 2** guidelines (September 2016) and relevant excerpts regarding living donor kidney transplantation. It is designed to provide Thai nephrology fellows with a comprehensive orientation to the legal, ethical, and clinical evaluation protocols for living kidney donors.

---

## 1. Legal and Ethical Framework for Living Donation

The foundation of living kidney transplantation in Thailand is governed by the Medical Council of Thailand (Regulations on Medical Ethics 2010) and the Thai Red Cross Organ Donation Centre.

### 1.1 Eligible Donor-Recipient Relationships
To prevent commercialization and ensure ethical standards, living donors must fall into specific categories:
*   **Genetic Relatives:** Parents, children, and full siblings. These must be provable via legal documentation or scientific evidence (HLA or DNA testing).
*   **Extended Relatives:** Uncles, aunts, nephews, nieces, and first cousins. These require DNA/HLA proof of relationship. If a dispute arises, the Thai Red Cross Organ Donation Centre serves as the arbiter.
*   **Legal Spouses:** Must have a marriage certificate and have been married for at least three years. 
    *   *Exception:* The three-year requirement is waived if the couple has biological children together. If the child's parentage is questioned, DNA proof is required.
*   **Foreign Nationals:** If the donor, recipient, or both are foreigners, documentation must be certified by their respective embassy or the Ministry of Foreign Affairs (MFA) and the Department of Consular Affairs. Genetic relationships must be confirmed by a Thai state medical institution using DNA/HLA testing.

### 1.2 Fundamental Ethical Rules
*   **No Commercialization:** Both donor and recipient must sign written declarations stating no payment or assets were exchanged for the organ.
*   **Voluntary Consent:** Donors must be fully informed of risks (surgical and long-term) and have the right to withdraw consent at any time before surgery.
*   **Standardized Care:** Evaluation must follow evidence-based protocols to minimize donor risk.
*   **Confidentiality:** Donor privacy must be protected, though relevant health data is shared for the benefit of the transplant pair.
*   **Independent Teams:** Ideally, the medical teams evaluating the donor and the recipient should be separate to avoid conflicts of interest.

## 2. Donor Evaluation and Clinical Triage

The evaluation process aims to identify the most suitable donor while ensuring the donor's long-term safety.

### 2.1 The Evaluation Timeline (Based on UK Guidelines adopted in TTC)
A systematic approach prevents unnecessary costs and donor inconvenience:
1.  **Referral (0–2 weeks):** Establish recipient fitness and initiate education.
2.  **Donor Identification (2–4 weeks):** Confirm ABO compatibility and initial HLA sensitization. Perform routine blood and urine tests to identify primary contraindications.
3.  **Coordination (2–4 weeks):** Multi-disciplinary discussion involving social, psychological, and medical risk factors.
4.  **Formal Assessment (Further 4 weeks):** Detailed "gold standard" investigative procedures.
5.  **Operation (5–7 weeks post-assessment):** Cross-match must be performed within 2 weeks prior to surgery.

### 2.2 Medical History and Physical Examination
Key areas of focus during the donor physical and history taking:
*   **Metabolic/Renal:** History of gout, nephrolithiasis, hematuria, proteinuria, and UTIs.
*   **Cardiovascular:** Hypertension, diabetes (including family history), atherosclerosis, and thromboembolic disease.
*   **Infection:** Tuberculosis, malaria, hepatitis, and HIV risk factors (e.g., tattoos/piercings within 6 months, high-risk sexual behavior).
*   **Malignancy:** Review of national screening results (mammography, cervical smears).
*   **Physical:** BMI, abdominal fat distribution, and blood pressure (measured at least twice).

## 3. Laboratory and Imaging Protocols

### 3.1 Renal Function Thresholds (TTC vs. KDIGO)
TTC 2 guidelines emphasize Glomerular Filtration Rate (GFR) adjusted for Body Surface Area (mL/min/1.73m²).

| GFR Level (mL/min/1.73m²) | Action Recommendation |
| :--- | :--- |
| **> 90** | Generally acceptable for donation. |
| **70 – 89** | Requires a detailed risk-benefit discussion. Consider age, comorbidities, and the donor's willingness to follow long-term monitoring. |
| **< 70** | Donor should be excluded. |

**Key Divergence:** TTC recommends calculating eGFR using the **2009 CKD-EPI** equation. If initial results are ambiguous, measured GFR (mGFR) using exogenous markers (e.g., Inulin, Iohexol, or 51Cr-EDTA) or a 24-hour creatinine clearance (averaged over 2 days) must be performed.

### 3.2 Proteinuria and Hematuria Assessment
*   **Proteinuria:** Prefer Albumin-to-Creatinine Ratio (ACR) or Urine Protein-to-Creatinine Ratio (UPCR) from a spot urine sample.
    *   **Exclusion Criteria:** Albumin Excretion Rate (AER) > 100 mg/day or UPCR > 300 mg/day.
*   **Hematuria:** Defined as ≥ 3–5 RBC/hpf in centrifuged urine.
    *   **Persistent Hematuria (2+ samples):** Requires workup for stones (US KUB), infection (Culture), or malignancy.

### 3.3 Routine Viral and Infectious Screening
All potential donors must be screened for:
*   Hepatitis B & C, HIV, and Syphilis.
*   CMV, EBV, and Toxoplasma.
*   **HTLV 1 & 2:** If appropriate/indicated.
*   **Varicella Zoster:** Specifically if the recipient is seronegative.
*   **Special Geographical Risks:** Malaria, Trypanosoma cruzi, Schistosomiasis, and HHV8 (if indicated).

## 4. Special Considerations in Donor Selection

### 4.1 The Elderly Donor
TTC does not set a strict "upper age limit," noting that centers globally have moved from limits like 55 toward 65 or higher. 
*   **Rationale:** Research indicates that while renal function declines with age, the surgical outcomes (operative time, blood loss, and length of stay) for donors > 60 years are comparable to younger donors, provided they are healthy.
*   **Requirement:** Both donor and recipient must be informed that an older kidney may have a shorter functional lifespan.

### 4.2 Anatomical Assessment
Imaging (US, CT Scan, or Radionuclide studies) is used to identify abnormalities.
*   **Kidney Size:** If there is a > 10% difference in size or function between the two kidneys, the **smaller or weaker** kidney should be taken for donation, leaving the better-functioning kidney with the donor.

## 7. Glossary of Important Terms

*   **ACR (Albumin-to-Creatinine Ratio):** The preferred method for evaluating urinary protein in potential donors; measured in mg/g.
*   **AER (Albumin Excretion Rate):** A measurement of albumin in a timed (usually 24-hour) urine collection; > 100 mg/day is an exclusion for donation.
*   **Banff Criteria:** (Contextual note) The international standard for histopathological classification of kidney transplant rejection.
*   **CKD-EPI 2009:** The standard equation recommended by TTC for initial GFR estimation based on serum creatinine.
*   **HLA (Human Leukocyte Antigen):** A protein marker used to determine tissue compatibility and to provide scientific proof of genetic relationship between donor and recipient.
*   **mGFR (Measured GFR):** The "gold standard" of renal function assessment using cleared substances like Inulin or Iohexol.
*   **Preemptive KT:** A transplant performed before the patient starts dialysis, which TTC aims to promote through planned living donation.
*   **TTC 2:** Thai Transplantation Care, version 2; the national guidelines for organ transplantation in Thailand.
