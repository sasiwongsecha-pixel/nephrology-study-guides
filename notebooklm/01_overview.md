# Thai Kidney Transplantation: Orientation and Living Donor Evaluation (TTC 2)

This briefing document provides an essential orientation for nephrology fellows regarding the current standards and regulatory frameworks for kidney transplantation (KT) in Thailand, primarily focusing on the **Thai Transplant Care (TTC 2)** guidelines for living donor evaluation.

## Executive Summary

Kidney transplantation is established as a critical intervention for end-stage renal disease (ESRD) in Thailand. The Thai Transplantation Society, in collaboration with the Ministry of Public Health and the Thai Red Cross Organ Donation Centre, emphasizes a rigorous evaluation process for living donors to ensure maximum benefit for recipients with minimum risk to donors. Given the chronic shortage of deceased donors in Thailand—where many potential donors are lost to accidents—the living donor program is a vital pillar of the national transplant strategy. Current guidelines prioritize evidence-based medical screening, legal and ethical transparency, and strict adherence to relationship-based donation to prevent commercialization.

## Analysis of Key Themes

### 1. Legal and Ethical Framework (Thai Medical Council & Thai Red Cross)
The legal landscape of KT in Thailand is governed by the **Medical Council's Regulations on Medical Ethics (2010)** and the **Thai Red Cross Organ Donation Centre (2002)**. These regulations are designed to prevent organ trafficking and ensure informed consent.

*   **Prohibition of Compensation:** Medical practitioners must ensure and document that no payment or benefits have been exchanged for the organ (TTC: Kidney, Page 8, 11).
*   **Informed Consent:** Donors must receive detailed explanations of the surgical risks and long-term health implications before signing a written consent form (TTC: Kidney, Page 8).
*   **Withdrawal Rights:** Donors maintain the absolute right to withdraw consent at any time prior to the surgery (TTC: Kidney, Page 11).

### 2. Donor Relationship Categories
To maintain ethical standards, the Thai system classifies eligible living donors into specific categories. If a relationship is in doubt, DNA or HLA testing is mandated for proof.

| Category | Requirements & Proof |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Genetic Relatives** | Includes parents, biological children, and siblings. Also extends to uncles, aunts, and first cousins (TTC: Kidney, Page 11). |
| **Legal Spouses** | Must be legally married or openly cohabiting for at least **3 years**. The 3-year requirement is waived if the couple has biological children together (TTC: Kidney, Page 7, 11). |
| **Foreign Nationals** | Requires certification from the donor/recipient’s embassy or government agency, further verified by the Department of Consular Affairs in Thailand (TTC: Kidney, Page 14). |
| **Swap/Exchange** | The regulations allow for "substituted" donation where an individual receives a transplant and then donates their removed organ (e.g., in specific medical contexts like domino transplants) (TTC: Kidney, Page 8). |

### 3. Medical Evaluation Standards (Thai-Specific Context)
While KDIGO provides a global baseline, the TTC guidelines outline specific thresholds for the Thai population regarding donor safety.

#### Renal Function (GFR)
The assessment must use the **2009 CKD-EPI** equation for eGFR.
*   **GFR > 90 mL/min/1.73 m²:** Acceptable for donation.
*   **GFR 70–89 mL/min/1.73 m²:** May be considered after intensive counseling regarding long-term risks, taking into account age and comorbidities.
*   **GFR < 70 mL/min/1.73 m²:** Generally excluded from donation (TTC: Kidney, Page 27).

#### Proteinuria and Hematuria
*   **Proteinuria:** Albumin Excretion Rate (AER) must be **< 100 mg/day** or total protein excretion **< 300 mg/day**. Measurements should ideally use ACR or UPCR (TTC: Kidney, Page 29).
*   **Hematuria:** Defined as **3–5 RBCs/hpf** in centrifuged urine. Persistent microscopic hematuria requires a full workup for stones, infection, or malignancy before the donor can proceed (TTC: Kidney, Page 31).

#### Donor Age
There is no absolute "upper age limit" in Thailand. However, kidneys from donors **over 60 years old** are classified as higher risk. Thai Transplant Registry data (1987–2015) shows that while only 2.04% of living donors are over 60, their surgical outcomes (blood loss, hospital stay) do not significantly differ from younger donors (TTC: Kidney, Page 23-24).

## Important Quotes with Context

> "The problem of kidney and organ shortage is a major issue in Thailand... although there are many brain-death cases from accidents, the number of organ donors remains low."
> — **Prof. Dr. Kriang Tantiwongse, President of the Thai Transplantation Society** (TTC: Kidney, Page 2)
*   **Context:** This highlights the motivation behind the TTC 2 guidelines: the urgent need to optimize the living donor program due to the insufficiency of the deceased donor pool.

> "The physician performing the transplant must explain to the donor the risks and dangers... both from the surgery itself and the long-term effects of living with one kidney."
> — **Section 1.2, Medical Council Regulations** (TTC: Kidney, Page 8)
*   **Context:** This emphasizes the "Donor First" ethical priority, ensuring that the healthy individual is not sacrificed for the benefit of the recipient without full understanding.

> "If the donor and recipient's kidneys are of unequal size (difference > 10%), it is recommended to remove the smaller or less functional kidney for donation, leaving the larger/better kidney for the donor's future health."
> — **Section 4.2, Evaluation of Kidney Function** (TTC: Kidney, Page 27)
*   **Context:** This is a specific Thai clinical guideline aimed at maximizing the long-term safety of the living donor.

## Actionable Insights for the Nephrology Fellow

1.  **Prioritize Pre-emptive Transplantation:** Fellows should identify potential recipients early and initiate discussions about living donation (ideally pre-emptive KT) to avoid the complications of long-term dialysis (TTC: Kidney, Page 15).
2.  **Verify Legal Standing Early:** Before initiating expensive medical workups, verify the legal relationship between donor and recipient. For spouses without children, ensure they meet the 3-year cohabitation/registration rule.
3.  **Strict GFR Monitoring:** Always use the CKD-EPI formula and adjust for Body Surface Area (BSA). If eGFR is borderline (70–89), proceed to a measured GFR (mGFR) using exogenous markers like Iohexol or 99mTc-DTPA (TTC: Kidney, Page 26).
4.  **Manage Donor Expectations:** When evaluating older donors (>60 years), provide specific counseling that while the surgery is safe for them, the "kidney age" may affect the long-term lifespan of the graft for the recipient (TTC: Kidney, Page 23).
5.  **Maintain Confidentiality:** Ensure that the donor's medical evaluation is kept confidential from the recipient to allow the donor a "medical exit" if they feel pressured to donate (TTC: Kidney, Page 15).
